SOC Incident Response Playbook

Standard corporate training manual for threat detection, checklist scoping, and psychological mitigation
01 // THE P-V-R THREAT HANDLING METHODOLOGY
1. PAUSE (Stop Before Action): Attackers rely on high urgency, fear, or corporate authority to override rational checking. If an email claims you must act within hours (e.g. password expire, penalty fine) or keep details secret, PAUSE immediately.

2. VERIFY (Scrutinize Sender and Requests): Inspect the actual email domain, not just the display name. Cross-check payment or security credentials requests using independent channels (phone directory, direct messaging, or walking to the office).

3. REPORT (Active Defense): Never delete or ignore a phishing mail. Forward details to your security team so standard filters can block lookalike domains and warn other coworkers.
02 // SCOPING THE CHECKLIST RED FLAGS
Our simulator checklist isolates **11 major indicators** used to flag alerts. Scoping details:
  • Sender mismatch [4 pts]: Display name matches standard executive or brand but the email header domain is completely unrelated (e.g., RK (CEO) <ceo-alert@external-gateway.com>).
  • Browser-in-the-browser (BitB) [4 pts]: Fake sign-in popups painted inside a compromised landing page. You cannot drag this window outside the main browser window frame.
  • Dangerous attachment [5 pts]: Files ending in extensions designed to run malicious scripts (e.g., .scr, .iso, .js, .exe, or double extensions like .pdf.scr).
  • Callback scam (TOAD) [3 pts]: Telephone-oriented attacks offering no links. The mail claims you have been charged and must call a support phone number to cancel, routing you to a voice scam.
03 // PSYCHOLOGICAL TRIGGER MATRIX
Social engineering bypasses technological controls by exploiting human psychology:

- Authority: Impersonating IT departments, regulatory agencies, executives, or government officers to enforce compliance.
- Urgency: Setting short deadlines (e.g. 2 hours, 24 hours) to prevent you from taking time to consult with security teams or managers.
- Fear-Greed: Threatening account suspension, data loss, tax penalties, or promising unrealistic high financial gain (e.g. high WFH salaries or ticket refunds).
- Curiosity: Promising allowance increases, benefits reviews, or interesting surveys to entice link clicking.
